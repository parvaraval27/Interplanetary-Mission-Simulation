Interplanetary Mission Simulation – Help

Reference guide for the mission planner parameters and outputs.

Inputs & Controls

Origin Planet / Destination Planet

These define the starting and target bodies of your mission. The simulator uses simplified circular orbits around the Sun with average orbital radii (in astronomical units, AU).

  • Inner planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars
  • Outer planets: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune

Payload Mass (kg)

The useful mass you want to deliver (spacecraft, instruments, cargo). Higher payload mass increases propellant requirements and total launch mass through the rocket equation.

Max Mission Duration (days)

The maximum allowed flight time. If the simulated travel time exceeds this limit, the mission is marked as Failed. Sliding the control fully right sets the duration to ∞ days, meaning there is no time constraint.

Propulsion System

Each card represents a propulsion model with different thrust and efficiency values:

  • Chemical Rocket: High thrust, lower specific impulse (Isp).
  • Electric Propulsion: Very efficient (high Isp) but low thrust, usually requires long mission durations.
  • Nuclear Thermal: Intermediate performance – higher Isp than chemical, better thrust than electric.
  • Solar Sail: Extremely high effective Isp (no propellant), but very low thrust and long trip times.

Trajectory Type

Chooses a simplified transfer model between orbits:

  • Hohmann Transfer: Classic two-burn, fuel-efficient transfer between nearly circular orbits.
  • Bi-elliptic Transfer: Uses an intermediate orbit; can be even more efficient than Hohmann for large radius changes, but often slower.
  • Brachistochrone: Idealized continuously-thrusting trajectory minimizing travel time, used here as a “fast but expensive” option.
Outputs & Metrics

Travel Time

Approximate mission duration in days, derived from the selected transfer orbit and propulsion assumptions.

Total Delta‑V (Δv)

The total change in velocity required for departure and arrival maneuvers, expressed in km/s. This is based on simplified two-body orbital mechanics and the chosen trajectory type.

Propellant Mass / Total Mass

Computed with the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation:

Δv = Isp × g₀ × ln(m₀ / mf)

  • Propellant Mass: Mass of fuel required to achieve the Δv.
  • Total Mass: Payload + propellant + structural mass.

Thrust‑to‑Weight (N/kg)

Effective thrust per unit mass. Higher values generally mean faster acceleration and potentially shorter transfer times, but this simulator keeps the underlying physics simplified.

Specific Impulse (Isp)

A measure of propulsion efficiency in seconds. Higher Isp means more Δv per unit of propellant mass. Electric and solar-sail systems have much higher Isp than chemical rockets.

Efficiency (%)

A simplified representation of how effectively each propulsion system converts propellant energy into useful Δv. This factors into the total Δv requirement and mass estimates.

Cost Estimate

A rough launch cost, assuming a constant cost per kilogram to low Earth orbit. This is intended as an order‑of‑magnitude indicator rather than an accurate budget.

Arrival Conditions

  • Approach Velocity: Spacecraft speed relative to the target planet at arrival.
  • Insertion Delta‑V: Δv needed to slow down into orbit around the destination.
  • Orbital Altitude: Target orbit height above the planet’s surface (km), sampled within a plausible range.

Mission Success

The mission is marked Success if both of the following are satisfied:

  • Travel time ≤ max mission duration (unless the duration slider is set to ∞).
  • Total mass is below a threshold limit (representing a practical launch mass cap).

If either constraint is violated, the mission is marked Failed and the mission status panel prompts you to adjust configuration.
